Introduction and Scope

This standard operating procedure (SOP) describes the procedures followed in the analysis of soils/sediments and waters for the determination of Total Petroleum Hydrocarbon (TPH) content by GC/FID at Manchester and by GC/FID & GC/MS at Warrington.

Due to the difficulties in sub-sampling liquids from sample containers that may contain floating product or particulates, sampling into a 'grab' bottle is strongly recommended. The laboratory then reports the result upon the whole content of the bottle.

The concentration range covered by the method will depend on the sample size and final extract volume. The linear range should lie between 10 and 20000 ng injected. Note that the lower limit of linearity is dependent on attainment of the necessary GC / GCMS response (as appropriate).

For the purposes of this method TPH is defined as all hydrocarbons extractable by the method within the carbon range from circa C 8 to circa C 35 . This range is appropriate for the determination of contamination caused by products such as diesel, fuel oils, heating oils, lubricating oils, paraffin/kerosene and white spirit. This method is not suitable for the analysis of gasoline range organics.

Speciation is performed by reference of the chromatograms obtained to those of standards of a number of petroleum products (see appendix C). When speciation is required a solution containing a range of n-Alkanes, ranging from C 10 to C 28 , is analysed to allow identification of carbon number ranges. This is the MDRO standard that is used for qualitative purposes only.

Hydrocarbon banding is performed by speciating the 'total' extracts into aliphatic and aromatic fractions as described ( Determination of TPH Aromatic Aliphatic Splits in the range C10-C35 ), currently there are four bands for each fraction, > C10-12, > C12-16, > C16-21, > C21-35. Matrix spike samples are processed at the same time.

It should also be noted that calibration is performed using a mineral oil mix. This standard is prepared as a 50:50 mix of Mineral Oil A (additive free diesel) and Mineral Oil B (additive free lubricating oil).

In real samples a wide range of aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbons may be present. Although a diesel standard is routinely used for analysis purposes, comparative analysis of a variety of fuels/oils has been performed and similar response diesel has been observed.

Principle

The method involves microwave, ultrasonic or liquid-liquid extraction of the test sample, followed by concentration, prior to analysis by capillary gas chromatography with flame ionisation or mass spectrometric detection. Quantitation is via the external standard method against diesel standards.

Method

UKAS Accredited

Method precision and accuracy, and hence uncertainty of measurement, is determined by six replicate analyses of a "sample" of known composition and routine Analytical quality Control (AQC).

MCERTS Accredited

Method precision and accuracy, and hence uncertainty of measurement was carried out in full compliance with the MCERTs Standard, version 3, March 2006 and associated briefing note 3, January 2007.

The method is an in-house protocol based on the standard methods referred to above in References.

Ongoing checks on method precision are provided via analyses of blank samples and matrix spiked samples. Additionally, standard B (5000ng/ul) shall be run at the beginning and end of every batch of analysis. The performance data obtained must agree with the limits of method precision outlined in Quality Control and AQC charts. If any data falls outside the performance limits then the samples shall be re-extracted or re-analysed. A CRM or spiked matrix sample is analysed on a monthly basis and the results plotted on AQC charts.

The method detection limit is normally 1 mg/kg, assuming a sample size of 10 g for solids, whilst for liquids the limit of detection is circa 0.05 mg/l, assuming a sample volume of 250 ml.

Soil/Sediment samples

Ultrasonic Extraction.

10 +/- 0.1 g of 'as received' sample is weighed directly into a 100 ml glass extraction jar/beaker. For samples that appear particularly oily the laboratory supervisor, section head, laboratory manager or laboratory director should be consulted. They will decide if a smaller aliquot should be taken for analysis. If this is the case the amount extracted should be clearly recorded on the SAL Sample Tracking Form

Add sufficient anhydrous sodium sulphate to the sample to form a dry powdery consistency.

Any excess sample is returned to Sample Reception and stored in the Surplus Sample Storage Area (see QP 17 Handling Storage and Disposal of Samples and Reagents ). Where necessary the excess sample is placed into clearly labelled re-sealable plastic bags prior to storage.

A 30 ml aliquot of DCM is added to the sample. The sample is then extracted in an ultrasonic bath for a period of at least three minutes, after which time it is removed and the particulates allowed to settle.

The solvent layer is decanted, through a Whatman type 541 filter paper, or equivalent, into a clean 250 ml beaker.

A further 30 ml aliquot of DCM is added to the sample, sonicated for a further three minutes and particulates allowed to settle. The solvent layer is decanted, through a Whatman type 541 filter paper, or equivalent, into a clean 250 ml beaker.

Sample clean up. Approximately 10 g of sodium sulphate is added to the 250 ml beaker, which is then swirled in both directions to allow sufficient mixing of the DCM with the sodium sulphate.

The DCM is then filtered through a Whatman type 541 filter paper, or equivalent, packed approximately half full with a layer of silica gel 60 which is then covered with sodium sulphate, into a clean round bottomed flask.

The DCM is then reduced in volume to < 5 ml, using a rotary evaporator. The extract is then transferred and concentrated to a final volume of 1.0 +/- 0.1 ml in a marked 2 ml vial.

Note: Vials must be capped immediately after extract transfer.

Note: The sample extracts should never be allowed to evaporate to dryness, if this occurs then the sample shall be re-extracted.

Microwave Assisted Extraction/TurboVap® Concentration

10 +/- 0.1 g of as received sample is transferred into a microwave extraction tube. For samples that appear particularly oily the laboratory supervisor, section head, laboratory manager or laboratory director should be consulted. They will decide if a smaller aliquot should be taken for analysis. If this is the case the amount extracted should be clearly recorded on the SAL Sample Tracking form.

Add sufficient anhydrous sodium sulphate to the sample to form a dry powdery consistency.

Any excess sample is returned to Sample Reception and stored in the Surplus Sample Storage Area (see QP 17 Handling Storage and Disposal of Samples and Reagents ). Where necessary the excess sample is placed into clearly labelled re-sealable plastic bags prior to storage.

A 30 ml aliquot of DCM is added to the sample, the tube is stoppered, capped tightly and shaken to ensure thorough mixing of the sample. The tube is then placed upon the microwave carousel. The microwave unit should be loaded with carousel placed correctly onto the plate so that its base is level with the floor of the microwave. Prior to starting an extraction cycle a check must be made to ensure that the solvent detection exhaust vents into the fume hood.

After ensuring the door of the microwave unit is securely closed select the preset extraction programme and leave the microwave until the programme is complete. The microwave programme parameters to be used are 60 °C and 75 % of 1200W (ca. 800 W) for at least 15 minutes.

At the conclusion of the cycle remove the carousel from the microwave unit, open the tubes and decant the solvent layer through a Whatman type 541 filter paper, or equivalent, packed approximately half full with a layer of silica gel 60, into a clean TurboVap® cylinder. The microwave tube shall be rinsed with ca. 10 ml DCM and this rinse shall be decanted through the filter.

Note: If the sample extract appears to contain residual moisture then additional sodium sulphate may be added to the filter along with the silica gel 60.

Check the TurboVap® to ensure that the temperature is 45 °C and that minimum water levels can be maintained for the duration of the cycle and that the Endpoint Select is set to sensor.

Load the cylinders into the TurboVap®, switch on the air compressor and start the concentration cycle.

Cylinders should be removed from the TurboVap® immediately at the conclusion of a cycle.

The extract is then transferred to a labelled 2 ml vial. The TurboVap® tube shall be rinsed with a small volume of DCM and this volume shall be transferred to the 2 ml vial. If required the final volume shall be adjusted to 1 ml under a gentle stream of air.

Note: Vials must be capped immediately after extract transfer.

Note: The sample extracts should never be allowed to evaporate to dryness, if this occurs then the sample shall be re-extracted.

If using a rotary evaporator, the extract is filtered as above into a clean round bottom flask and evaporated to ca. 2-3 ml. The temperature of the evaporator water bath shall not exceed 40°C and no vacuum shall be used whilst evaporating. The evaporated extract is then transferred to a 2 ml autosampler vial and evaporated to a final volume of 1.0 ml under a gentle stream of air.

Aqueous samples

Aqueous samples should be supplied to the laboratory in separate 250 ml 'grab' bottles. In situations where this is not the case and a sub-sample from a larger bottle is required, a 250 ml aliquot decanted from the bottle after settlement of particulates is used. Separatory Funnel Extraction

For a 'grab' sample, empty the contents into a 500 ml measuring cylinder and record the volume of the sample. Wash the sample bottle twice with 10 ml of DCM and add to the separatory funnel to be used for the sample extraction. Pour the sample from the cylinder into the separatory funnel and rinse the cylinder with 10 ml of DCM adding the rinsing to the separatory funnel. Proceed with the extraction. Note that if a significant amount of floating product or sediment is present in a sample record this on the tracking form.

Where a sub sample from a larger bottle is required, decant 250 +/- 5 ml of sample, after settlement of particulates, into a 500 ml measuring cylinder and transfer into a separatory funnel. Rinse the cylinder with 10 ml of DCM and add the rinsing to the separatory funnel. Proceed with the extraction. Note that if a significant amount of floating product or sediment is present in a sample record this on the tracking form.

Where a customer requests an LOD of 10 mg/l a volume of 1000 ml of test sample is required for extraction.

The sample is liquid/liquid extracted by shaking with two separate 30 ml aliquots of DCM, the solvent layers are collected in a clean 250ml beaker. Sample clean up. Approximately 10 g of sodium sulphate is added to the 250 ml beaker, which is then swirled in both directions to allow sufficient mixing of the DCM with the sodium sulphate.

The DCM is then filtered through a Whatman type 542 filter paper, or equivalent, packed approximately half full with a layer of silica gel 60 which is then covered with sodium sulphate, into a clean round bottomed flask.

Note: If the sample emulsifies then it will be necessary to centrifuge the sample in order to separate the organic and aqueous phases.

The DCM is then reduced in volume to < 5 ml, using a rotary evaporator. The extract is then transferred and concentrated to a final volume of 1.0 +/- 0.1 ml in a marked 2 ml vial.

Note: Vials should be capped immediately after extract transfer.

Note: The sample extracts should never be allowed to evaporate to dryness, if this occurs then the sample shall be re-extracted

Wash the sample bottle twice with 10 ml of DCM and add to the bottle to be used for the sample extraction. Pour the sample from the cylinder into the bottle and rinse the cylinder with 10 ml of DCM adding the rinsing to the bottle. Proceed with the extraction. Note that if a significant amount of floating product or sediment is present in a sample record this on the tracking form.

Note: that if a significant amount of floating product or sediment is present in a sample record this on the tracking form.

Once the stirrer has finished, close the tap on the solvent decanter, invert the glass bottle and allow all the solvent to settle at the bottom of the inverted bottle and decant the solvent layer through a Whatman type 541 filter paper, or equivalent, packed approximately half full with a layer of silica gel 60 which is then covered with sodium sulphate, into a clean TurboVap® cylinder.

Check the TurboVap® to ensure that the temperature is 50 °C and that minimum water levels can be maintained for the duration of the cycle and that the Endpoint Select is set to sensor.

The extract is then transferred to a labelled 2 ml vial. The TurboVap® tube shall be rinsed with a small volume of DCM and this volume shall be transferred to the 2 ml vial. If required the final volume shall be adjusted to 1 ml under a gentle stream of air.

C10-C35 Aromatic / Aliphatic Splits

Prepare samples as detailed in the Soil Sediment samples section of this SOP or Aqueous samples section of this SOP as applicable . Note: It may be necessary to reduce the final volume of the extract to ca.100ul to reduce the volume of dichloromethane in the extract.

The required number of Matrix Spike samples are prepared as described in the section for Quality Control and processed as described below in the same manner as the test samples. The matrix spike recovery for each fraction is plotted on the relevant quality control charts.

Precondition a pre-packed Phenomenex Strata SPE column with 20mls DCM followed by 20 ml hexane. The DCM must be allowed to pass fully onto the column before proceeding to add the hexane. Discard these washings.

Transfer the TPH extract directly to the top of the cartridge, rinse the vial with approximately 2 ml of hexane and transfer this wash to the top of the column. Allow the column to stand for 2-3 minutes in order for the aromatic fraction to fully absorb onto the column.

Elute the ALIPHATIC fraction using 20 ml hexane.

Elute the AROMATIC fraction using 20 ml of DCM.

Concentrate both fractions to 1 ml, transfer to a labelled autosampler vial and pass to the analyst.

NOTE: Care must be taken to ensure that the extract does not become completely dry as this may lead to the loss of naphthalene.

For GC/MS analysis

Quality Control

A method blank shall be prepared for each batch of analysis. For water samples 250 +/-10 ml of deionised water shall be used and for soil samples 10 +/-0.1g of sodium sulphate shall be used. In general for results to be accepted the blank concentration should not be greater than the limit of detection. However, if the sample concentrations are over 100 times greater than the blank then the blank contribution shall be deemed negligible and the results acceptable. Additionally, if the hydrocarbon pattern seen in the blank is different to that in the samples then the blank contribution can be ignored.

For each batch of analysis a matrix spike shall be prepared. The matrix spike is prepared by spiking 250 ml +/-10 ml of deionised water or 10 g +/-0.1g of control soil with 500 ul +/-5 ul of the stock standard (10000 ng/ul). This spiking should be performed at least 30 minutes prior to extraction to allow assimilation of the spike with the sample matrix. This gives concentrations of 532 mg/kg and 21.3 mg/l, respectively. All percentage recoveries shall be plotted on the relative quality control chart, i.e. for Banded aliphatic/aromatic splits the % recovery for each band will be plotted.

Note that the matrix spike should not be analysed immediately after the calibration. It is normal practice for it to be at least the fifth sample in the sequence.

After no more than 20 samples, or at the end of the batch, re-analyse standard B. The result for standard B must be within +/- 15 % (Manchester/Warrington) and 6.6 % (East Kilbride) of its true value for data to be valid. If the error is larger then all the samples must be re-analysed.

Qualitative Assessments

Where a speciated TPH is required the results are normally reported in one of two ways.

The carbon range detected is reported, e.g. "from circa C12 to C20 peaking at C16".

The MDRO standard must be analysed if speciation is required to enable the carbon range to be established. The chromatograms for the samples and the MDRO standard should be compared to allow the 'peak' and 'range' of the hydrocarbon profiles to be reported based upon carbon number. This procedure must only be performed by suitably trained and qualified staff.

A qualitative assessment of the hydrocarbon type is made based upon reference to the chromatograms of standards. Chromatograms of a series of commercial products are given in Standard Chromatograms - Modified Diesel Range Organics .

C6-C35 Aromatic / Aliphatic Splits

Soil/Sediment Samples

10 +/- 0.1 g of the sample `as received` is weighed directly into a 40 ml vial.

Add 2 +/- 0.1 ml of methanol and shake for approximately 5 minutes.

Add 10 +/- 0.1 ml of pentane and shake for approximately 5 minutes and allow to settle. If necessary add 1-2 ml of deionised water to aid the separation.

Column Chromatography

A chromatography column is prepared as follows. A glass chromatography column (300 mm x 10.5 mm id) is plugged with a small amount of cleaned glass wool. The column is packed with, from the bottom, separate layers of: 8 g activated silica gel (100-120 mesh); 1 g activated alumina (100-250 mesh, Brockmann type 1, neutral). The column is pre-washed with 30 ml pentane. Discard the eluate. Columns may be prepared and stored until required for use, however when this is the case, they are not pre-washed until immediately prior to use.

Check the column for air bubbles or channelling. If present, tap the column to remove.

The crude sample concentrate is dissolved in 1 ml of pentane. This - together with two further 5 ml pentane rinses - is applied to the top of the pre-washed column, taking care that each pentane rinse has passed onto the column prior to introducing the next rinsing. After the sample has been applied, the column is eluted with 20 ml pentane. The eluate is collected in a 100 ml beaker marked with SAL sample number and 'aliphatic fraction'.

The solvent system is now changed to pentane:dichloromethane (DCM) 50:50 (v/v). The beaker which contained the crude sample concentrate is rinsed with 5 ml of pentane:DCM 50:50 (v/v) and this - together with two further 5 ml rinses - is applied to the top of the column. The column is eluted with 20 ml pentane:DCM 50:50 (v/v). The eluate is collected in a 100 ml beaker marked with the SAL sample number and 'Aromatic Fraction'.

The collected fractions are concentrated to incipient dryness in a fume hood and then transferred to 2 ml vials in dichloromethane. The solvent is then allowed to evaporate at ambient temperature.

Note: Care should be taken not to allow the extract to become completely dry as naphthalene may be lost.

Resuspend all extracts in 1.0 ml (+/- 0.05 ml) of DCM and cap the vial.

Aqueous Samples

30 +/- 0.3 ml of the sample is transferred to a 40 ml vial.

Add 3 +/- 0.1 ml of pentane and shake for approximately 5 minutes.

Continue as described Column Chromatography

GC Temperature Profile

Use the same GC temperature profile as GC Calibration set-up and Quality Control , when analysing for C6-C35 aromatic/aliphatic splits except that the start temperature should be 35 o C

Initial Oven Temperature: 35 o C, held for 3 min,

Ramp Rate: 15 o C/minute,

Final Temperature: 310 o C, held for 6 minutes.
